What "Cosigner" Actually Means in California

The bail bond cosigner — also called the indemnitor in legal language — signs an indemnity agreement with the bondsman that creates two simultaneous obligations:

  • Financial: Pay the 10% premium fixed under California Insurance Code §1800.4. This is non-refundable, regardless of case outcome.
  • Contractual: Take responsibility for ensuring the defendant attends every scheduled court appearance. If the defendant skips court, the cosigner is liable for the FULL bail amount (not just the 10%).

The cosigner also typically authorizes the bondsman to hire a bail recovery agent if the defendant absconds — and the cosigner is on the hook for recovery costs (typically 10-25% of the bail amount, often $5,000-$25,000) in addition to the original bail.

Your Two Layers of Liability

Layer 1: Premium (always paid)

10% of the bail amount. Non-refundable. Paid up front or via payment plan. On a $50,000 bail, that is $5,000 paid to the bondsman regardless of how the case ends — dismissal, plea, conviction, acquittal.

Layer 2: Forfeiture (paid only if defendant fails to appear)

Under California Penal Code §1305, if the defendant fails to appear in court, the bond is forfeited and the bondsman is liable to the court for the FULL bail amount. The bondsman then pursues the cosigner via the indemnity agreement. On a $50,000 bail, the cosigner's worst-case exposure is $50,000 — plus recovery agent costs, plus court forfeiture costs.

Key insight: When you cosign for a $50,000 bail, you are NOT just promising $5,000. You are promising up to $50,000+ if the defendant skips court. Read the indemnity agreement carefully and only cosign for someone you trust to appear at every hearing.

What Triggers Forfeiture

The bond is forfeited under PC §1305 when:

  • Defendant fails to appear at a scheduled court hearing
  • Defendant violates a condition of release that constitutes a new crime
  • Defendant absconds from the jurisdiction (leaves California or country without court permission)
  • Defendant is arrested on a new charge that triggers automatic bond review

The bondsman has 180 days under PC §1305(b) to either (a) bring the defendant back to court, or (b) pay the full bail amount to the court. During this window, the bondsman pursues all means to locate the defendant — and the cosigner is the primary financial backstop.

How Collateral Works

For higher bail amounts (typically $50,000+), the bondsman may require collateral in addition to the cosigner's signature. Common collateral types:

  • Real estate — typically equity in a primary residence or rental property; recorded as a lien
  • Vehicles — title held until bond exonerated
  • Cash deposit — held in escrow until bond exonerated
  • Jewelry, electronics, other valuables — physically held by bondsman

Collateral is released back to the cosigner when:

  1. The defendant completes all scheduled court appearances
  2. The case fully resolves (dismissal, plea, sentence served, or acquittal)
  3. The bond is exonerated by the court

This typically takes 3-9 months for misdemeanors, 6 months to 2+ years for felonies. The collateral is at risk during the entire pendency.

Before You Sign — 5 Cosigner Questions

  1. Do I trust this person to appear at every court date? If not, do not cosign. The financial exposure is too large.
  2. Is the bail amount within my realistic financial capacity if forfeited? Don't cosign for $100K bail if losing $100K would devastate your finances.
  3. Have I read the indemnity agreement carefully? Especially the forfeiture conditions, recovery agent fees, and collateral terms.
  4. Has the bondsman explained late fees, payment plan terms, and collateral release timing in writing? If not, ask before signing.
  5. Can the defendant comply with the bail conditions? CPO terms, travel restrictions, drug/alcohol testing — if compliance is unlikely, forfeiture risk is high.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does a cosigner actually agree to?
Two obligations: pay the 10% premium (CA Ins Code §1800.4), and ensure the defendant attends every court appearance. If defendant skips, cosigner liable for FULL bail amount under PC §1305.
What is my actual financial liability?
Premium (always paid, 10%) + Forfeiture (only if defendant fails to appear, full bail amount + recovery costs). On $50K bail, worst-case = $50K+, not $5K.
Can I get my collateral back?
Yes — released when defendant completes all appearances, case resolves, and bond is exonerated. Typically 3-9 months for misdemeanors, 6 months to 2+ years for felonies.
What happens if the defendant misses court?
PC §1305 — court issues bench warrant + declares bond forfeited. Bondsman has 180 days to return defendant or pay full bail. Cosigner pursued via indemnity agreement.
